Reinforcement learning (RL) is a subset of machine learning algorithms that involve an agent and an environment. The goal is to train the agent to take actions in its environment that maximize its return. Some common applications of RL are training the computer to play video games, trade stocks, or drive a car. The basic premise of training a computer to perform these human-like tasks is very similar to using positive reinforcement from psychology.

Think about training a dog to perform a trick. The end goal is for the furry canine to complete the entirety of the trick. If the trick is an obstacle course, the trainer would want to give the dog a treat every time it completes part of the course, relaying to the dog it is on the right track and it should go further next time for more treats. If the dog fails the obstacle in front of it, the trainer will not give the dog a treat. Eventually, the dog learns which actions earn it a treat and which ones do not, leading to the dog completing the course. The computer works in a very similar fashion.

Let us say we want to train the computer to drive a virtual car through a racetrack. The agent is the car, and the environment is the course. The end goal for the agent is to cross the finish line. Initially, the computer has no idea how to drive a car or what even the end goal is; thus it will start off by pressing random buttons. So, we may want to provide the computer with a reward every time it goes forward. This teaches the computer that going forward will increase its reward more than going backwards. We may also want to give the computer a negative reward if it turns around or if it goes off the track. This teaches the computer to avoid taking such actions. We also may give it a negative reward each second it doesn't finish, enforcing the idea that speed is also a factor that needs to be considered. Finally, we may give it a very large reward if it does end up crossing the finish line, which finishes the iteration. Additionally, if there are obstacles on the racecourse, we can give the computer a large negative reward if it collides with any of them, leading to an early termination of the iteration. As each training iteration progresses, the computer will slowly become more confident in what actions to take to maximize its rewards; It'll start taking less random actions and more of the actions it deems as fruitful. After thousands of training iterations, the computer will have learned how to navigate the car through the racetrack by taking the actions that'll grant it positive rewards and avoiding the ones that'll grant it negative rewards.
